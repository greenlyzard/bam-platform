# BAM Level System
## docs/claude/LEVEL_SYSTEM.md — Placement & Level Guide

---

## Overview

BAM uses a two-part level system: a **number** for age bracket and a **letter** for skill within that bracket. This means a parent asking "what level is my 9-year-old who has danced for two years?" gets a specific, confident answer — not "try a few classes and see."

The system exists to protect students: a child placed too low is bored; placed too high, they struggle and quit. Right placement is the single most important factor in a student's first-year retention.

---

## Number System — Age Brackets

| Level | Age Range | Description |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **Petites** | 3–5 years | Pre-technique. Creative movement, imaginative play, basic coordination. No formal barre or center work. Pure joy of moving. |
| **Level 1** | 5–7 years | First formal technique. Simple barre exercises, basic positions, across-the-floor. Short attention spans — class structure is playful but purposeful. |
| **Level 2** | 7–10 years | Structured technique. Proper turnout, basic center combinations, beginning turns and leaps. Split into A/B/C skill tracks. |
| **Level 3** | 10–13 years | Advancing technique. Multiple pirouettes, complex combinations, performance focus. Competition-eligible at 3C. |
| **Level 4** | 13+ years | Company-level training. Full-length repertoire, pointe work (ballet), pre-professional preparation. Most intensive track. |
| **Teen/Adult** | 14+ | All skill levels welcome. Non-competitive, welcoming environment. Separate beginner and intermediate tracks. |

---

## Letter System — Skill Within Age Group

| Letter | Skill Level | What It Means |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| **A** | Beginner | New to formal technique in this age group. Starting or restarting. No prior class history required at this level. |
| **B** | Intermediate | Solid foundation established. Advancing combinations, improving consistency, ready for more complex choreography. |
| **C** | Advanced | Near-company level. Technique is reliable under performance pressure. Competition-eligible. Leadership role in class. |

## Placement Guidelines

### By Age Alone (no prior dance)
| Child's Age | Start Here |
|-------------|-----------|
| 3–4 years | Petites |
| 5 years | Level 1 |
| 6–7 years | Level 1 |
| 8–9 years (never danced) | Level 2A |
| 10–12 years (never danced) | Level 3A |
| 13+ years (never danced) | Level 4B or Teen/Adult Beginner |
| 14+ adult (never danced) | Teen/Adult Beginner |

### By Prior Experience
| Experience | Placement Guidance |
|------------|-------------------|
| 1 year at another studio | Same number, drop one letter (e.g., trained as "intermediate" → start at A in that number) |
| Competition background | May test into letter C — assessment recommended |
| BAM transfer (returning student) | Resume previous level unless gap > 1 year |
| Gap of 1–2 years | Drop one letter, same number |
| Gap of 3+ years | Drop to A in same number bracket |

## Level Progression Timeline

Typical progression (varies by student, not guaranteed):

```
Petites (1–2 years)
    ↓
Level 1 (1–2 years)
    ↓
Level 2A → 2B → 2C (1 year each)
    ↓
Level 3A → 3B → 3C (1–2 years each)
    ↓
Level 4B → 4C (ongoing, company track)
```

Advancement is based on teacher evaluation, not age alone. A student stays at their level until they're ready — there is no automatic promotion.

## Common Parent Questions

**"My daughter did dance at [other studio] for 3 years. Where does she go?"**
→ Ask her age and what level she was called at that studio. If they don't use letters, ask what she was working on (single pirouettes? pointe? combinations?). Use that to map to BAM's system. When genuinely unclear, recommend the free assessment class.

**"My son is 11 and has never danced. Is it too late?"**
→ Never too late. Start at Level 3A — designed for this exact situation. Many Level 3A students began at 10–12 with zero background.

**"Can she skip levels if she's really good?"**
→ Teachers assess and recommend promotion. It happens, but BAM prioritizes solid foundations over fast tracking. A student who rushes to 4C before they're ready gets injured or discouraged.

**"What's the difference between 2B and 2C?"**
→ Primarily consistency and complexity. 2B students are building; 2C students have it built and are refining. 2C is often the most competitive class in that number group.
