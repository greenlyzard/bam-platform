# Teacher Onboarding Program
## Ballet Academy and Movement

> **Purpose:** Prepare every new instructor to teach with the skill, philosophy, and professionalism that defines Ballet Academy and Movement.  
> **Duration:** 2 weeks before first solo class + ongoing mentorship

---

## 1. Onboarding Philosophy

At Ballet Academy and Movement, every teacher represents Amanda Cobb's training philosophy and the studio's reputation in the community.

New teachers don't just learn *what to teach* — they learn *how to see* students, how to create a room that feels safe and encouraging while demanding excellence, and how to make every child feel both challenged and capable.

This is not a checklist. It's a craft apprenticeship.

## 4. BAM Teaching Philosophy — Core Principles

Every teacher at Ballet Academy and Movement teaches according to these principles:

### 1. Technique Serves Expression
Corrections exist to help the body express the music, not to make the student conform to a shape. Always explain *why* a correction matters.

### 2. Encouragement and Standards Are Not Opposites
A child can be told "that's not quite right yet" in a way that makes them want to try harder — or in a way that makes them afraid. Our teachers always choose the former.

### 3. Every Child Is a Complete Dancer Now
Even a 3-year-old in Tiny Dancers is a dancer — not a "future dancer" or a "student someday." Treat every student as already belonging to the art form.

### 4. The Room Sets the Tone
The teacher's energy, posture, and presence when the first student walks in determines the energy of the entire class. Arrive focused. Arrive ready.

### 5. Small Classes Demand Full Attention
With a maximum of 10 students, every child is visible in every moment. There is no hiding at the barre. Use this — make every student feel seen.

### 6. Parents Are Partners
Parents watching in the waiting area or lobby are watching us. Our manner with children is always professional, warm, and deliberate.

### 7. Progress Is Not Linear
Some students progress faster visually. Some students are building internal understanding that will bloom later. Do not write off the slow bloomer.

## 5. Age Group Guides

### Tiny Dancers (Ages 2-3)
- Class structure: 30-45 minutes, heavy imagination-based movement
- Primary goal: Enjoy being in a class, follow simple direction, experience music
- Technique goals: None yet — body awareness and spatial sense
- Common challenge: Short attention spans, separation anxiety
- Approach: Songs, stories, props, "be a butterfly" framing — not "do this position"
- Parent interaction: Parents often in the room at this age; involve them naturally

### Pre-Ballet (Ages 3-5)
- Class structure: 45 minutes, half imagination/play, half beginning positions
- Primary goal: Love ballet; learn first positions without them knowing it
- Technique goals: First position feet, port de bras beginnings, simple pliés
- Common challenge: Wanting to do everything their older sibling does
- Approach: "Good feet means your heels are together, like a butterfly" — use imagery

### Ballet Foundations (Ages 5-7)
- Class structure: 45-60 minutes, barre + center + across the floor
- Primary goal: Begin formal vocabulary; musicality emphasis
- Technique goals: Plié, tendu, dégagé, basic relevé, port de bras
- Common challenge: Some students from recreational backgrounds have bad habits to gently undo
- Approach: Direct and technical, but never harsh. "Let's try that again, this time..."

### Classical Ballet I-III (Ages 7-12)
- Class structure: 60-75 minutes, full barre + center + combinations
- Primary goal: Build correct classical vocabulary; musicality; physical conditioning
- Technique goals: Vary by level — see curriculum guide
- Common challenge: Students who compare themselves to peers; perfectionists
- Approach: Individualized feedback; name effort as much as result

## 6. Classroom Management

### Starting Class
- Start on time, every time. Lateness is addressed privately after class, never in front of peers.
- Begin at the barre — this creates immediate structure and focus.
- Greet every student by name.

### During Class
- Never raise your voice at a child. Raise your presence instead.
- If a student is being disruptive: redirect with specific task ("Can you show everyone how?"), not shame.
- If a student is crying: stop, acknowledge, give them a moment. Do not force participation.

### Ending Class
- Always end with a formal reverence (bow/curtsy). This is non-negotiable at every class, every age.
- End on time. Parents waiting in the lobby are trusting us with their schedule.
- Walk students to the lobby — never let young children exit alone.

## 7. Communication with Parents

### What to Share
- Progress updates proactively (positive and constructive)
- Any physical concerns (pain, injury risk, developmental notes)
- Performance or competition recommendations

### How to Share
- In person after class (brief, warm)
- Via the platform communication system (M1) for longer updates
- Never via personal cell phone or personal social media

### What Not to Do
- Do not discuss one student's progress (or struggles) with other parents
- Do not make placement or promotion decisions without Amanda's input
- Do not promise a student a role or performance slot without Amanda's approval

## 8. Mandated Reporter Responsibilities

Every teacher at Ballet Academy and Movement is a mandated reporter under California law.

**You are legally required to report if you have reasonable suspicion of:**
- Physical abuse
- Sexual abuse
- Emotional abuse / severe neglect
- Self-harm risk in a student
- Bullying that rises to a safety concern

**Reporting process:**
1. Do not investigate — report your observation only
2. Do not tell the student's parent before reporting
3. Report immediately to Amanda Cobb (studio director)
4. Amanda will coordinate report to the appropriate authority
5. In Amanda's absence: call 1-800-540-4000 (California Child Abuse Reporting Hotline) directly
6. Document what you observed (date, time, what you saw or heard) in writing

**You cannot be held liable for a good-faith report that turns out to be unfounded.**  
**You can be held liable for failure to report.**

All teachers must complete California's AB 1432 mandated reporter training before their first class.
